# Shot Selection & Repertoire

## Straight Drive (Rail / Length)

The straight drive is the most fundamental and important shot in squash. A well-executed straight drive travels tight to the side wall, deep into the back corner.

### Execution
- **Aim**: Hit the front wall approximately 30–60 cm above the tin, close to the side wall
- **Target area**: The ball should hug the side wall and die (second bounce) near the back wall
- **Pace**: Medium to hard — the ball should carry deep but not bounce off the back wall into mid-court
- **Height**: Higher drives are safer (more margin above the tin), lower drives are more attacking

### When to Use
- **Default shot** — when in doubt, hit a tight straight drive
- Under pressure from the back of the court
- When the opponent is on the T and you need to push them deep
- To build rallies and establish control

### Quality Indicators (Visible on Video)
- Ball travels close to the side wall (within 30 cm)
- Ball reaches the back wall or dies in the back corner
- Opponent is forced to play from the back corner
- The ball does not come back to the middle of the court

### Common Errors
- **Hitting to the middle**: Drive goes to center court — gives the opponent an easy volley
- **Too low**: Ball hits the tin — unnecessary risk
- **Too short**: Ball bounces twice before the back quarter — the opponent intercepts easily
- **Off the side wall**: Ball comes off the side wall into the middle — easy for the opponent

## Crosscourt Drive

A crosscourt travels from one side of the court to the other, passing through the middle.

### Execution
- **Aim**: Hit the front wall at an angle that sends the ball to the opposite back corner
- **Width**: The ball should pass wide enough that the opponent cannot volley it from the T
- **Depth**: Should reach the back quarter of the court on the opposite side
- **Height**: Usually medium height — enough to pass over the opponent's volley reach

### When to Use
- To change the angle and move the opponent laterally
- When the opponent is off-center (e.g., stranded on one side)
- To set up a following straight drive (wrong-footing the opponent)
- As a variation to prevent the opponent from anticipating straight drives

### Common Errors
- **Too narrow/short**: Ball passes through the middle — opponent volleys it easily
- **From the back corner**: Crosscourting from the back is risky — the ball travels a long way and the opponent has time to intercept
- **Telegraphing**: Open body position clearly shows the crosscourt intention — no deception

**Coaching cue:** "Crosscourts should hit the opposite side wall BEHIND the service box — if it lands in front, it's a gift to your opponent"

## Boast (Angle Shot)

A boast hits the side wall first, then the front wall. It moves the opponent to the front of the court.

### Types
- **Two-wall boast (standard)**: Side wall → front wall. The main attacking/repositioning boast
- **Three-wall boast**: Side wall → front wall → opposite side wall. More deceptive, tighter to the nick
- **Trickle boast**: Very soft boast from the front of the court — barely reaches the front wall, dies near the nick
- **Skid boast (reverse boast)**: Hits the OPPOSITE side wall first — the ball goes crosscourt. Deceptive and risky
- **Back-wall boast (defensive)**: When trapped deep, hitting the back wall → front wall as a last-resort retrieval

### When to Use
- To bring the opponent forward when they're positioned deep
- As a variation after establishing a length game
- When the ball is behind you in the back corner (often the only option)
- Trickle boast from the front when the opponent is behind you

### Common Errors
- **Boasting too frequently**: Predictable — opponent reads it and covers the front early
- **Boasting from mid-court**: Gives the opponent too much time; only boast from deep in the court
- **Too hard**: Ball comes off the front wall too far, giving the opponent an easy shot

## Drop Shot

A soft shot aimed to die near the front wall, as close to the tin as possible.

### Types
- **Straight drop**: Drops tight to the side wall near the front — the most effective drop
- **Crosscourt drop**: Travels to the opposite front corner — riskier, more deceptive
- **Volley drop**: A drop played out of the air — takes time away from the opponent

### When to Use
- When the opponent is deep in the back of the court
- After establishing length to create the expectation of another drive
- When you have time and good positioning (DON'T drop under pressure)
- From the front of the court when the opponent is behind you

### Quality Indicators
- Ball stays very low (just above the tin)
- Ball stays tight to the side wall
- Ball's second bounce is before the short line
- Opponent cannot reach it or is forced into a weak retrieval

### Common Errors
- **Dropping under pressure**: When stretched or off-balance — the drop will be loose and sit up
- **Too high above the tin**: Ball bounces up and the opponent has an easy counter-attack
- **Telegraphing**: Slowing down obviously before the drop — opponent reads it
- **Hitting the tin**: Going too low — the highest-risk error

## Lob

A high, soft shot that travels in an arc to the back of the court, often used defensively.

### Types
- **Defensive lob**: High shot from the front when under pressure — buys time to recover
- **Attacking lob (crosscourt)**: High, wide crosscourt that pushes the opponent deep and to the side
- **Straight lob**: Travels high and tight to the side wall, deep into the back corner

### When to Use
- **Under pressure at the front** — the safest option to reset the rally
- To change the pace when the opponent is dominating with hard, fast shots
- To push the opponent deep when they're camped at the T
- As a variation after drops and drives to disrupt rhythm

### Quality Indicators
- Ball reaches the back quarter of the court
- Ball hits the side wall near the back corner (making retrieval difficult)
- Opponent cannot volley it (too high and too wide)
- The rally is "reset" — both players return to neutral positions

### Common Errors
- **Too low**: Opponent volleys it easily — turns your defensive shot into their attacking opportunity
- **Too short**: Ball doesn't reach the back — opponent attacks from mid-court
- **Not wide enough**: Crosscourt lob passes through the middle — easy intercept

**Coaching cue:** "When in trouble, go UP — a high lob gives you 2 seconds to recover to the T"

## Volley

A shot played before the ball bounces — intercepting it out of the air.

### Why Volleys Are Critical
- **Takes time away from the opponent** — they have less time to recover to the T
- **Maintains court position** — the volleyer stays near the T instead of retreating to the back
- **Keeps pressure on** — volleys keep the tempo high and the opponent under pressure

### Types
- **Volley drive**: A firm, punched volley deep into the back corner
- **Volley drop**: A soft, deceptive touch volley that dies at the front
- **Volley kill**: A low, hard volley aimed at the nick or tin area
- **Volley crosscourt**: Redirecting the ball to the opposite side out of the air

### When to Use
- Whenever the ball passes through the mid-court area at a reachable height
- Especially on loose crosscourts from the opponent
- When the opponent is behind you — volleying keeps you in front

### Common Errors
- **Letting volleable balls go**: Retreating to the back instead of intercepting — loses positional advantage
- **Over-hitting volleys**: Swinging too hard — volley technique is about timing and placement, not power
- **No racket prep for volleys**: Because volleys happen fast, racket must ALREADY be up
- **Volleying everything**: Some balls are not meant to be volleyed (e.g., tight, fast drives along the wall)

## Kill Shot

A low, hard shot aimed to die in or near the nick (the join between the floor and the side wall).

### When to Use
- When the ball sits up (loose ball) in the front half of the court
- When the opponent is deep in the back — they have maximum distance to cover
- Only when the player has good balance and time — NEVER when stretched or rushed

### Common Errors
- **Going for kills from the back** — the ball has too far to travel and the opponent has time to cover
- **Attempting kills under pressure** — results in tins and unforced errors
- **Too ambitious**: Aiming for the nick every time instead of just keeping it low

## Serve

### Types
- **Lob serve (default)**: High, soft serve that lands deep in the back corner — the safest and most common serve
- **Hard serve (power serve)**: Low, fast serve aimed at the opponent's body or the back wall nick
- **Body serve**: Aimed directly at the receiver's body — awkward to return, especially for beginners

### Serve Strategy
- **Vary the serve**: Don't always serve the same way — keep the receiver guessing
- **Lob serve is safest**: High margin, pushes the receiver deep, hard to attack
- **Hard serve as surprise**: Occasional hard serve catches the receiver off guard

### Common Serve Errors
- **Short serve (doesn't pass the short line)**: Fault — point to the opponent
- **Serving to the middle**: Gives the receiver an easy volley return
- **Same serve every time**: Predictable — the receiver grooves their return
