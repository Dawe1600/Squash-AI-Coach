# Footwork & Court Movement

## The T-Position (Central Position)

The T-position is the intersection of the half-court line and the short line — the most strategically valuable position on the squash court. Controlling the T means:
- **Minimum distance** to every corner of the court (approximately 3–4 steps to any point)
- **Maximum visual coverage** — the player can see both the ball and the opponent
- **Offensive readiness** — the player can volley intercepting balls or push forward to attack short balls

### Recovery to the T
After every shot, the player MUST recover back toward the T. This is the single most important habit in squash footwork.

**Signs of poor T-recovery (visible on video):**
- Player stays rooted in the corner after hitting
- Player watches the ball travel to the front wall instead of moving
- Player drifts too far forward or backward, leaving gaps
- Player is consistently caught out of position for the opponent's return

**Coaching cues:**
- "Hit and move — never admire your shot"
- "Imagine a rubber band pulling you back to the T after every shot"
- "Your feet should start moving BEFORE the ball hits the front wall"

## Split-Step (Ready Hop)

The split-step is a small, explosive hop or bounce performed just as the opponent is about to strike the ball. It is the foundation of reactive movement.

### Mechanics
1. **Timing**: Initiate the hop approximately 0.3–0.5 seconds BEFORE the opponent's racket makes contact
2. **Execution**: A small, balanced hop (feet shoulder-width apart, landing on the balls of the feet)
3. **Landing**: Land in a slightly crouched, athletic stance with knees bent (~30°)
4. **Purpose**: Pre-loads the leg muscles (stretch-shortening cycle), enabling explosive first-step acceleration in any direction

### Common Split-Step Errors
- **Too late**: Landing after the opponent has already hit — player is still in the air and cannot react
- **Too high**: Jumping too high wastes time and energy; the split-step should be barely visible (~2–5 cm off the ground)
- **Flat-footed**: Not performing a split-step at all — the player is "stuck" and reacts slowly
- **Too narrow/wide stance**: Reduces the ability to push off explosively in the required direction

**Coaching cues:**
- "Light bounce on your toes just before they hit — feel the court spring back"
- "Think of it as loading a spring — small bounce, huge reaction"

## Ghosting Patterns (Movement Without the Ball)

Ghosting is the practice of moving to each corner of the court and simulating a shot, then recovering to the T. There are 6 primary ghosting points:

1. **Front-left** (forehand front corner for right-handers)
2. **Front-right** (backhand front corner for right-handers)
3. **Mid-left** (forehand side wall, volley position)
4. **Mid-right** (backhand side wall, volley position)
5. **Back-left** (forehand back corner)
6. **Back-right** (backhand back corner)

### Movement Principles
- **Push off from the T** using the leg closest to the direction of travel (crossover step or side-step)
- **Accelerate to the ball**, decelerate into the shot with controlled footwork
- **Hit with a stable base** — front foot pointing toward the side wall (not the front wall)
- **Push back** to the T using the front leg as the driving leg

## Lunge Technique

The lunge is the final step to reach a ball that is far from the player's current position.

### Proper Lunge Mechanics
- **Leading leg**: The leg closest to the ball extends forward
- **Knee alignment**: Front knee should NOT extend past the toes (injury risk)
- **Low center of gravity**: Bend deeply at the knee and hip
- **Racket extension**: Arm extends fully to reach the ball
- **Push-back**: Use the front leg to push explosively back toward the T

### Common Lunge Errors
- **Reaching with the arm instead of stepping** — leads to off-balance shots and no power
- **Knee caving inward** (valgus collapse) — injury risk for the ACL
- **Not bending the back knee** — leads to an upright, weak lunge position
- **No push-back** — player stays stuck at the front of the court after the lunge

## Level-Specific Footwork Issues

### Beginner
- Does not return to the T after shots (most common and most critical error)
- Stands flat-footed — no split-step or ready position
- Runs directly at the ball in a straight line (no efficient movement pattern)
- Crosses feet instead of using side-steps or crossover steps
- Uses too many steps to reach the ball (inefficient)

### Intermediate
- Returns to the T but too slowly (walks instead of quick-stepping)
- Split-step timing is off (too early or too late)
- Lunge technique is shallow — doesn't get low enough
- Over-runs the ball (ends up too close, crowding the shot)
- Neglects ghosting the backhand back corner

### Advanced
- Late recovery under pressure (doesn't push back immediately after a defensive shot)
- Split-step becomes lazy during long rallies (fatigue)
- Doesn't adjust movement pattern against deceptive opponents
- Front-foot positioning angle is inconsistent (sometimes faces the front wall instead of side wall)
