# Common Mistakes by Player Level

This document organizes the most frequently observed squash errors by skill level. Each error includes what it looks like on video, why it matters, and the priority level for coaching feedback.

---

## Beginner Level

Beginners typically have 0–12 months of experience. Their primary issues are foundational movement and basic shot execution.

### Critical Errors (Address Immediately)

#### B1: No T-Recovery
- **What it looks like**: Player hits the ball and stays in the corner, watching it travel. Does not move back toward the center of the court.
- **Why it matters**: The player is always out of position for the next shot, constantly chasing the ball reactively.
- **Visual cue**: Player is in the back corner while the opponent plays from a comfortable T position.
- **Category**: footwork

#### B2: No Racket Preparation
- **What it looks like**: Player runs to the ball with the racket hanging by their side or down at waist level. Only lifts the racket at the last second.
- **Why it matters**: No time for a proper swing — shots are weak, inaccurate, and have no deception.
- **Visual cue**: Arm and racket rise simultaneously in a rushed upward motion just before contact.
- **Category**: racket_technique

#### B3: Flat-Footed / No Split-Step
- **What it looks like**: Player stands upright with weight on heels, not bouncing or ready to move. No visible hop or bounce when the opponent hits.
- **Why it matters**: Delayed first step — the player is always a half-second late getting to the ball.
- **Visual cue**: Player appears "stuck" or "frozen" before reacting to the opponent's shot.
- **Category**: footwork

#### B4: Crowding the Ball
- **What it looks like**: Player runs directly at the ball and ends up too close. Elbow is jammed against the body during the swing.
- **Why it matters**: No room for a proper swing — shots are cramped, weak, and inaccurate.
- **Visual cue**: Player's body is right on top of the ball at contact, arm bent at a sharp angle.
- **Category**: racket_technique

### Moderate Errors

#### B5: Hitting to the Middle
- **What it looks like**: Drives and crosscourts consistently land in the center of the court rather than tight to the walls.
- **Why it matters**: Opponent can volley from the T or play comfortably from mid-court.
- **Visual cue**: Ball trajectory passes through or near the T area.
- **Category**: shot_selection

#### B6: Watching the Ball After Hitting
- **What it looks like**: Player hits and then stands watching the ball, only starting to move when the opponent returns.
- **Why it matters**: Wasted time — the player should be recovering to the T during the ball's flight.
- **Visual cue**: Player is stationary for 1–2 seconds after their shot.
- **Category**: positioning

#### B7: Using Frying Pan Grip
- **What it looks like**: Racket face is flat/horizontal instead of vertical. Player hits with the face of the racket facing the floor/ceiling.
- **Why it matters**: Limits shot variety, removes wrist action, creates predictable shots.
- **Visual cue**: At contact, the racket face appears flat rather than perpendicular.
- **Category**: racket_technique

## Intermediate Level

Intermediate players (1–3 years) have basic shots but struggle with consistency, tactics, and movement efficiency.

### Critical Errors

#### I1: Lazy Split-Step
- **What it looks like**: Player does a split-step but too late, too high, or too flat. The split-step doesn't actually help the first-step acceleration.
- **Why it matters**: The split-step is only effective if timed correctly — a late split-step is worse than none.
- **Visual cue**: Player appears to bounce AFTER the opponent has already hit, rather than landing AS they hit.
- **Category**: footwork

#### I2: Crosscourting from the Back Corner
- **What it looks like**: Player hits crosscourts from the back of the court. The ball travels a long diagonal through the middle.
- **Why it matters**: The opponent has ample time to intercept (volley) from the T. Crosscourts from the back are one of the most punished errors.
- **Visual cue**: Ball travels from the back corner on one side to the opposite side, passing through or near the T.
- **Category**: shot_selection

#### I3: Over-Hitting Drops
- **What it looks like**: Drop shots that bounce too high, sit up, or land in the middle of the front quarter.
- **Why it matters**: A bad drop is an invitation for the opponent to attack. Drops must be tight and low to be effective.
- **Visual cue**: Ball bounces noticeably above the tin and the opponent can play a comfortable counter-shot.
- **Category**: shot_selection

### Moderate Errors

#### I4: Slow T-Recovery
- **What it looks like**: Player does return to the T, but walks slowly instead of quick-stepping. Arrives at the T just as or after the opponent hits.
- **Why it matters**: Late arrival at the T means the split-step is late, and the player is always half a step behind.
- **Visual cue**: Player is still walking toward the T when the opponent strikes the ball.
- **Category**: footwork

#### I5: Telegraphing Shots
- **What it looks like**: Player's body position clearly shows what shot they're going to play (e.g., opening the body wide for a crosscourt, slowing down obviously for a drop).
- **Why it matters**: The opponent reads the shot early and is already moving to the right position.
- **Visual cue**: Opponent starts moving to the correct position BEFORE the player has hit the ball.
- **Category**: tactical

#### I6: Not Volleying When Possible
- **What it looks like**: Ball passes through mid-court at a reachable height, but the player lets it go and retreats to the back.
- **Why it matters**: Missing volley opportunities surrenders T-dominance and lets the opponent settle.
- **Visual cue**: Ball passes within arm's reach of the player who is near the T, but they step back.
- **Category**: tactical

#### I7: Inconsistent Spacing
- **What it looks like**: Sometimes too close (crowding), sometimes too far (reaching). No consistent distance from the ball.
- **Why it matters**: Inconsistent spacing = inconsistent shot quality. The player can't groove reliable technique.
- **Visual cue**: Arm position at contact varies wildly — sometimes jammed, sometimes fully stretched.
- **Category**: racket_technique

## Advanced Level

Advanced players (3+ years) have solid fundamentals but need refinement in high-pressure execution and tactical nuance.

### Critical Errors

#### A1: Late Racket Prep Under Pressure
- **What it looks like**: When stretched or under time pressure, the player reverts to late racket preparation — running first, preparing second.
- **Why it matters**: Under pressure is exactly when early prep matters MOST — it's the difference between a controlled shot and a scrappy retrieval.
- **Visual cue**: In fast rallies, the player's racket is visibly not ready when they arrive at the ball.
- **Category**: racket_technique

#### A2: Poor Width on Crosscourts
- **What it looks like**: Crosscourts pass too close to the T area, allowing the opponent to volley.
- **Why it matters**: At advanced levels, any ball through the middle is punished immediately.
- **Visual cue**: Opponent intercepts crosscourts with volleys from or near the T.
- **Category**: shot_selection

### Moderate Errors

#### A3: Unnecessary Risk-Taking
- **What it looks like**: Going for kills, nicks, or tight drops from difficult positions instead of playing safe length.
- **Why it matters**: Unforced errors are the biggest point-losers at advanced levels. Shot selection discipline wins matches.
- **Visual cue**: Multiple tin errors or loose attacking shots from defensive positions.
- **Category**: tactical

#### A4: Fatigue-Induced Technique Breakdown
- **What it looks like**: As the match progresses, movement becomes slower, split-step disappears, racket prep drops, follow-through shortens.
- **Why it matters**: Maintaining technique under fatigue is what separates good players from great players.
- **Visual cue**: Noticeable decline in movement quality and shot execution as the session continues.
- **Category**: footwork

#### A5: Over-Reliance on Favorite Shots
- **What it looks like**: Player repeatedly goes to the same shot patterns (e.g., always drops from the forehand front, never boasts).
- **Why it matters**: Predictability at advanced levels is punished — opponents adjust quickly.
- **Visual cue**: Repeated identical shot patterns with the opponent increasingly covering them.
- **Category**: tactical
