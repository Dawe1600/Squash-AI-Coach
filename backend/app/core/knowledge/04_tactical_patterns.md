# Tactical Patterns & Strategy

## T-Dominance (Court Control)

The fundamental strategic principle of squash: **the player who controls the T controls the rally.**

### What T-Dominance Looks Like
- Player returns to the T (or near it) after every shot
- Player intercepts balls early with volleys from the T
- Opponent is constantly pushed to the corners and back wall
- Player dictates the pace and direction of the rally

### How to Achieve T-Dominance
1. **Hit length** — tight, deep straight drives push the opponent behind you
2. **Volley aggressively** — cut off the opponent's returns from the T
3. **Move the opponent** — alternate between front and back, left and right
4. **Recover quickly** — don't linger after your shot; sprint back to the T

### Signs of Lost T-Dominance (Visible on Video)
- Player is consistently hitting from behind the T
- Player is chasing the ball rather than positioning proactively
- Opponent volleys frequently while the player retrieves from the back
- Player is off-center, leaving gaps on one side

## The Length Game (Building Pressure)

The length game is the backbone of winning squash strategy. It means consistently hitting the ball deep into the back corners to pin the opponent behind you.

### Strategy
- **Objective**: Hit tight straight drives and deep crosscourts to the back quarter of the court
- **Goal**: Force the opponent to hit from a weak position (back corner), creating loose balls for you to attack
- **Patience**: Length is not glamorous, but it wins rallies. The player who is more patient with length usually wins

### The "Build-and-Attack" Pattern
1. **Build phase**: 3–5 tight length shots to push the opponent deep
2. **Wait for the opening**: Opponent eventually hits a loose ball (short, to the middle, or off the wall)
3. **Attack phase**: Play a drop, volley drop, or kill shot to the front

### When Length Is Failing
- Drives are going to the middle instead of tight to the wall
- Drives are too short — bouncing in mid-court
- Opponent is volleying your drives — need to go higher or tighter
- Player is impatient — going short too early before building pressure

**Coaching cue:** "Win the back wall, win the rally — make your opponent HIT from the back before you go short"

## Width Game (Moving the Opponent Laterally)

While length moves the opponent front-to-back, width moves them side-to-side.

### Strategy
- Use crosscourts to make the opponent move laterally
- Alternate straight drives with crosscourts to create angular movement
- Wide crosscourts force the opponent off the T to retrieve from the side wall

### Width Patterns
- **Straight → Crosscourt → Straight**: Classic pattern to wrong-foot the opponent
- **Crosscourt from the front → Straight from the back**: Changes the angle dramatically
- **Wide crosscourt lob**: Pushes the opponent deep AND wide simultaneously

### Common Width Errors
- Crosscourts that travel through the middle (not wide enough)
- Predictable crosscourt patterns (opponent anticipates and volleys)
- Using crosscourts from the back corner (risky — the ball travels too far)

## Attacking from the Front

When the ball is in the front quarter of the court, the player has several attacking options.

### Shot Selection from the Front
| Opponent Position | Best Shot | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Deep in back corner | Straight drop or trickle boast | Maximum distance for opponent to cover |
| On the T | Hold the ball and disguise (drop or drive) | Force the opponent to commit early |
| Moving forward | Counter-drop or lob over them | Exploit their forward momentum |
| Off to one side | Straight drive past them | They can't cover the straight ball |

### Deception from the Front
- **Hold the shot**: Keep the racket up and delay the swing to make the opponent guess
- **Show one shot, play another**: Shape for a drive, play a drop (or vice versa)
- **Use the wrist**: Late wrist adjustments can redirect the ball at the last moment

## Defensive Play (Under Pressure)

When the player is stretched, off-balance, or trapped in a corner, defensive play is essential.

### Defensive Priorities (In Order)
1. **Get the ball back** — any return is better than losing the point
2. **Go high** — a lob gives maximum time to recover
3. **Go tight** — hug the side wall to make the opponent's next shot harder
4. **Recover to the T** — immediately move back after the defensive shot

### The Lob as a Defensive Weapon
- When under pressure at the front, a high crosscourt lob:
  - Buys 2–3 seconds of recovery time
  - Pushes the opponent deep
  - Resets the rally to a neutral position
- **Do NOT try to hit winners under pressure** — this leads to tins and unforced errors

### Common Defensive Errors
- **Going for a risky shot when stretched** — hitting the tin or leaving a loose ball
- **Not lobbing high enough** — the opponent volleys your "defensive" shot
- **Not recovering after the defensive shot** — standing and watching instead of moving

**Coaching cue:** "When you're in trouble, there's only ONE shot: lob high, lob deep, recover to the T"

## Tempo Changes (Pace Control)

Varying the pace of the rally is a subtle but powerful tactical tool.

### Fast Tempo
- Hard drives, quick volleys, low kills
- **Purpose**: Rush the opponent, give them no time to set up
- **When to use**: When the opponent is slow, when you're dominating the T, when the opponent likes slow rallies

### Slow Tempo
- High lobs, soft drops, slow-paced drives with height
- **Purpose**: Break the opponent's rhythm, frustrate power players
- **When to use**: Against fast-paced aggressive opponents, when fatigued (buys recovery time), to set up a sudden tempo change

### Tempo Change Pattern
- Play 3–4 hard, fast drives → suddenly play a soft, slow drop or lob
- The sudden change catches the opponent mid-stride, expecting another hard shot
- The "gear change" is one of the most effective tactical weapons in squash

## Pressure-and-Attack Sequences

These are multi-shot patterns that build toward a winning shot.

### Pattern 1: Length → Short
1. Hit 3–5 tight straight drives to the back corner
2. Wait for the opponent's drive to be slightly loose (short or off the wall)
3. Play a tight drop or volley drop to the front

### Pattern 2: Straight → Crosscourt → Volley
1. Hit tight straight drives to establish the pattern
2. Play a deep crosscourt to move the opponent laterally
3. The opponent's return often comes through the middle — volley it for a winner

### Pattern 3: Boast → Counter-drop → Lob
1. Play a boast from the back corner to bring the opponent forward
2. Opponent plays a counter-drop — you're already at the T
3. Play a high lob over the opponent to push them deep again

### Pattern 4: Serve → Volley Attack
1. Hit a high, deep lob serve to the back corner
2. Move to the T immediately
3. Opponent's return is often loose from the back — volley it aggressively
