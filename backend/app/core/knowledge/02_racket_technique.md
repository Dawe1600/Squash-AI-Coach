# Racket Technique

## Early Racket Preparation (Racket Prep)

Early racket preparation is the habit of bringing the racket up and back BEFORE running to the ball. This is arguably the single most impactful technical improvement a player can make.

### Why It Matters
- **Time advantage**: The swing only has to go forward, not back-and-then-forward
- **Deception**: When the racket is up early, the opponent cannot read the shot direction until the last moment
- **Power**: Full backswing allows a longer acceleration path
- **Accuracy**: More time to line up the shot, less rushed execution

### What "Ready" Looks Like
- Racket head is above shoulder height
- Elbow is bent at approximately 90°
- Wrist is cocked back slightly
- Non-racket hand helps balance and can point toward the ball for alignment

### Common Racket Prep Errors
- **Late preparation**: Running to the ball with the racket down by the side — forced to "arm" the ball with no swing
- **Low preparation**: Racket is back but at waist level instead of shoulder level
- **Only preparing on one side**: e.g., good forehand prep but lazy backhand prep
- **Dropping the racket between shots**: Racket head drops to knee level during movement, requiring extra time to raise it again

**Coaching cues:**
- "Racket up FIRST, then run — not the other way around"
- "Show me the strings to the back wall before you start your swing"
- "Think: prepare while you're still on the T, not when you arrive at the ball"

## Grip

### Continental (Chopper) Grip
The standard squash grip — used for the majority of shots:
- Hold the racket as if shaking hands with it
- The V between thumb and index finger sits on the top-left edge of the handle (for right-handers)
- This grip allows natural forehand AND backhand shots with minimal grip adjustment

### Pan-Handle Grip
- The racket face is held flat/parallel to the ground
- Used occasionally for specific shots (e.g., straight kills from the front, some boasts)
- SHOULD NOT be the default grip — limits versatility and wrist action

### Common Grip Errors
- **Too tight grip**: Causes arm tension, reduces wrist snap, accelerates fatigue — grip should be firm but relaxed
- **Rotating grip between shots**: Constantly adjusting grip wastes time and introduces inconsistency
- **Frying pan grip as default**: Restricts shot variety and creates predictable, flat shots

**Coaching cues:**
- "Hold the racket like you're holding a bird — firm enough not to let it fly, gentle enough not to crush it"
- "Shake hands with the racket — that's your grip"

## Swing Path & Follow-Through

### The Ideal Swing
1. **Backswing**: Racket is high and back (the preparation phase)
2. **Downswing**: Racket drops slightly, then accelerates forward in a smooth arc
3. **Contact point**: Racket face meets the ball at approximately hip-to-knee height, slightly in front of the leading foot
4. **Follow-through**: Racket continues forward and upward, finishing high above the opposite shoulder

### Open vs. Closed Racket Face
- **Open face**: Racket face tilted slightly upward — produces loft (lobs, defensive shots)
- **Closed face**: Racket face angled slightly downward — produces flat, hard drives and kills
- **Neutral/square face**: Standard for drives — the ball travels straight and true

### Follow-Through Patterns
- **High follow-through**: Finishes above shoulder — indicates a full, powerful swing (drives, crosscourts)
- **Compact follow-through**: Short, controlled finish — used for drops, touch shots, volleys
- **No follow-through**: Stopping the racket abruptly at contact — leads to chopped, weak shots with no control

### Common Swing Errors
- **Chopping/slicing**: Cutting across the ball instead of swinging through it — the ball has no power or direction
- **Wristy flick with no swing**: Using only the wrist instead of the full arm — inconsistent and weak
- **Chicken wing elbow**: Elbow sticks out sideways during the swing, reducing reach and power
- **Hitting behind the body**: Contact point is too far back — the ball goes crosscourt unintentionally or hits the side wall

**Coaching cues:**
- "Swing THROUGH the ball, not AT the ball"
- "Finish high — your racket should end up over your opposite shoulder"
- "Contact the ball in front of your leading knee, not behind your hip"

## Spacing (Distance from the Ball)

Spacing is the distance between the player's body and the ball at the moment of contact. Correct spacing is critical for generating power and accuracy.

### Optimal Spacing
- **Arm's length plus a few inches** — the arm should be slightly bent at contact, NOT fully extended or jammed close to the body
- The front foot should be approximately 30–50 cm from the ball's bounce point
- The player should feel comfortable and balanced, not reaching or cramped

### Crowding (Too Close)
When the player is too close to the ball:
- The elbow is forced to bend sharply, reducing swing arc
- Power is lost because there's no room for a full swing
- Accuracy drops — shots tend to spray or hit the tin
- Common cause: running directly at the ball instead of getting alongside it

### Over-Reaching (Too Far)
When the player is too far from the ball:
- The arm is fully extended and locked — no wrist action possible
- Balance is compromised — the player is off-center
- Recovery to the T is slower because the player is stretched out

**Coaching cues:**
- "Get alongside the ball, not behind it — like you're standing next to it, not chasing it"
- "If your elbow is jammed into your ribs, you're too close"
- "Create space — the ball doesn't bite, give it room"

## Level-Specific Technique Issues

### Beginner
- No racket preparation — runs first, then lifts the racket at the last moment
- Death grip (too tight) — arm fatigues quickly, no wrist action
- Frying pan grip used for all shots
- Hitting with a "punching" motion instead of a smooth swing
- Contact point is inconsistent (sometimes in front, sometimes behind)

### Intermediate
- Racket prep on forehand but not on backhand
- Follow-through is inconsistent — sometimes high, sometimes cut short
- Crowding the ball on straight drives (positioning too close)
- Wrist flick on volleys instead of a compact, controlled volley swing
- Swing path changes under pressure (becomes rushed and choppy)

### Advanced
- Late racket prep under high-pressure rallies (when stretched or fatigued)
- Slight grip inconsistencies between forehand and backhand transitions
- Over-reliance on wrist for deception at the cost of consistency
- Follow-through shortens during long matches (fatigue-induced)
